# Quick Guide: Problem Framing

**File:** `prompts/problem-framing.md`
**Phase:** Pre-charter (optional)
**Mode:** Solo fill — do not open a Claude session for this
**When to use:** You're entering a problem space where you lack domain intuition. Skip if you already understand the user, the pain, and the landscape.

---

## Fields

**Domain** — The space you're exploring, not the solution you're imagining.

**Who has this problem?** — Describe a real person in a real moment. What are they doing when the problem hits? What tools do they have open? Personas are abstractions — this should be concrete.

**What is the problem?** — State it from the user's perspective ("I can't ___", "I spend too long ___"), not yours ("the market lacks ___"). If you can't write this sentence, you don't yet understand the problem well enough to build for it.

**How do they solve it today?** — Workarounds, manual processes, competitors, ignoring it. The current solution reveals the real constraints your product inherits.

**Competing hypotheses** — Two or more possible product responses. They need to be distinct, not good. The concept brief narrows to one.

**What evidence supports or undermines each?** — Label personal experience as such. "I did this job for three years" is evidence. "Users probably want mobile" is a guess. Both are valid entries — the label matters.

**What you don't know** — Gaps carry forward into the concept brief as assumptions to test.

---

## What happens next

Fill this, then move to `concept-brief.md` to narrow to one direction. When you paste `concept-challenge.md` into a Claude session, attach the completed problem framing alongside the concept brief — Claude uses the competing hypotheses and evidence to run a stronger adversarial review.
